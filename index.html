Resume & ATS Keywords

98% of Fortune 500 companies use ATS software that ranks your resume before any human sees it. ATS scores you on keyword match. The core rule: show, don't tell — tool + action + measurable result, every bullet.

The formula: [Strong action verb] + [specific tool or method] + [measurable result with a number]

The most important before/after examples

❌ Flagged by ATS & humans
"I'm a hard worker and fast learner."
✓ Specific · searchable · provable
"I edit high-converting TikTok videos for e-commerce brands using CapCut and Premiere Pro."
❌ Weak opener
"Responsible for managing social media."
✓ Action + metric
"Grew Instagram from 2K to 18K followers in 6 months, A/B testing captions and posting 5×/week."
❌ Task, not achievement
"Helped with customer service tasks."
✓ Tool + scale + outcome
"Resolved 40+ daily tickets via Zendesk, maintaining a 95% satisfaction rating."
❌ Pure filler
"Proven track record of driving results."
✓ Named metric
"Increased email open rates from 18% to 34% by rewriting subject lines and segmenting lists."
❌ Vague
"Worked on various marketing campaigns."
✓ Specific + dollar amount
"Launched 3 Facebook ad campaigns targeting 25–40 yr demographics, generating $12K in direct sales."
❌ Generic leadership claim
"Led a team of people to success."
✓ Size + outcome + timeline
"Directed a 7-person team to deliver a product relaunch 3 weeks ahead of schedule, generating $80K in first-month revenue."

ATS formatting rules (the invisible gatekeepers)

  • ✕No tables, columns, or text boxes.ATS converts to plain text first — columns get scrambled, images become garbled characters, your resume becomes unreadable.
  • ✕Never keyword-stuff unnaturally.Modern ATS uses AI to detect unnatural language. Cramming 30 keywords into one paragraph reads as spam and lowers your score.
  • ✕One resume for every job = low ATS scores.52% of target-job keywords are absent from the average unoptimised resume. Each application needs tailoring.
  • ✓Mirror the job posting's exact language.If it says "stakeholder management" — write exactly that, not "managing relationships". ATS matches exact terms.
  • ✓Include a dedicated Skills section listing tool names explicitly (Salesforce, not "CRM software"; Python, not "programming language").
  • ✓Add a number to every achievement.%, $, time saved, team size, volume — any metric is infinitely better than no metric.
  • ✓Use .docx or .pdf only.Never .pages, .txt, or Google Docs links. Many ATS systems only parse Word/PDF properly.

LinkedIn Optimisation

87% of recruiters use LinkedIn as their primary sourcing tool. Profiles with optimised headlines and complete sections are 40× more likely to receive opportunities. Recruiters spend just 6 seconds scanning — your headline and first 2 lines of your About section are your entire first impression.

Headline — your most important 220 characters

Formula: [Role/Expertise] | [Specialisation or Key Skill] | [Value Signal or Remote/Location note]

❌ Wasted space
"Marketing Manager at ABC Company"
✓ Searchable + specific + value
"B2B Content Strategist | SaaS & Fintech | Turning blogs into pipeline | Open to Remote"
❌ Desperation signal
"Remote | WFH | Work From Home | Flexible | Seeking Opportunities"
✓ Skill first, availability second
"Full-Stack Developer (React + Node) | Building scalable apps | Remote-ready, UTC+1"
❌ Entry-level with no direction
"Recent Graduate | Looking for Opportunities"
✓ Entry-level with specificity
"Data Analyst | Python & Tableau | Turning raw data into clear decisions | Open to remote & hybrid"

About section (your 2,600-character story)

  • ✓Write in first person, conversationally."I help e-commerce brands..." reads human. "Seasoned professional with extensive experience..." reads like a template recruiter ignores.
  • ✓Open with your clearest value statement in the first two lines — that's all that shows before "see more". Make those lines count.
  • ✓End with a call to action."Feel free to reach out at [email]" or "Open to conversations about [role type]." Tell people exactly what to do next.
  • ✕Don't copy-paste your resume summary.The About section is for your story, personality, and context — not a bulleted list of job duties.
  • ✕Avoid buzzwords even here."Passionate team player with a proven track record" is just as hollow in your About section as on a resume.

Profile completeness checklist

  • Professional headshot (face fills ~60% of frame — photos generate 14× more views)
  • Custom banner image that reinforces your field or personal brand
  • Optimised headline using the formula above — not just your job title
  • About section in first person with a CTA
  • All experience entries have bullet points with metrics — not just job titles and dates
  • Skills section with 10+ relevant, searchable skills listed
  • At least 2–3 recommendations from managers, clients, or collaborators
  • Open to Work turned on (use "Recruiters only" to avoid alerting your current employer)
  • Custom URL — linkedin.com/in/yourname not linkedin.com/in/abc12345xyz
  • Featured section with portfolio piece, article, case study, or key project

Activity that boosts visibility

  • iPost or comment at least once a month.Active engagement signals relevance to LinkedIn's algorithm and increases how often your profile appears in recruiter searches.
  • iCommenting thoughtfully outperforms liking by a significant margin in algorithm terms — and warms cold connections before you reach out.
  • ✕Don't doom-scroll and compare.Seeing others' career wins amplifies self-doubt and wastes job-search hours. Use LinkedIn for outreach and posting, then close the tab.

Cover Letter

A cover letter answers the question a resume can't: "Why this company, why now, and why you?" Keep it under 350 words. One page max. Every sentence should either prove a skill or show you understand the company.

The 4-paragraph structure: (1) Why this company specifically. (2) Your best relevant achievement with a number. (3) Why you're the right fit for this type of role. (4) Confident close with a CTA.

Templates for each paragraph

Opening — show you researched them (not generic)
"I've followed [Company]'s work in [specific area] — particularly [specific project, value, or recent news]. That focus is exactly why I'm applying for the [Role] position."
Achievement paragraph — with a number
"In my previous role at [Company], I [strong verb + specific outcome + metric]. I achieved this by [brief how — method or tool used]."
Fit paragraph — remote version
"I've worked remotely [for X years / since X] and am fluent in [Slack, Notion, Zoom, Asana]. I default to over-communication and async documentation to keep distributed teams aligned."
Fit paragraph — onsite / local version
"I'm based in [City] and genuinely excited about joining a team I can work alongside in person. I thrive in collaborative, face-to-face environments and am available to start [timeframe]."
Confident close — never desperate
"I'd welcome the chance to discuss how my background in [specific skill] could contribute to [Company]'s goals. I'm available for a call at your convenience."

Cover letter rules

  • ✓Address a real person by name.Check LinkedIn or the company site. "Dear Hiring Manager" is fine if you truly can't find a name. Never "Dear Sir/Madam."
  • ✓Focus on what you bring them, not what you want."I'm looking for growth" = you want something. "I can help you do X" = you offer something. Employers care about the second one.
  • ✓Name the specific role in the first sentence — companies often hire for multiple positions simultaneously, and copy-paste errors (wrong company name) are instant rejections.
  • ✕Never repeat your resume.The cover letter provides context and motivation. The resume lists facts. They should complement — not duplicate — each other.
  • ✕Avoid buzzwords even here."I'm a passionate, results-driven professional" is as hollow in a cover letter as on a resume.
  • ✕Don't explain gaps or weaknesses unprompted.The cover letter is not the place to apologise. Save that for when — if — they ask.
  • ✕Never send the same letter to multiple companies.Generic cover letters are immediately obvious. Even small tweaks — one specific detail, the company name — signal genuine interest.

Interview — Say This, Not That

Interviewers form an impression in the first 90 seconds. Use the STAR method for every experience question — it keeps answers specific, structured, and prevents rambling.

The STAR method (the only framework you need)

LetterMeaningExample
SituationSet the brief context"Our client retention had dropped 20% over two quarters."
TaskYour specific role"I was tasked with identifying why and rebuilding our onboarding."
ActionWhat YOU did (not "we")"I interviewed 12 churned clients, rebuilt the onboarding flow in Notion, and trained the team."
ResultMeasurable outcome"Retention improved by 34% in the following quarter."

Phrases that impress interviewers

  • ✓"I'm not familiar with that tool, but I'm eager to learn — I'd take a course and be up to speed within [timeframe]." Honesty + resourcefulness. Far better than bluffing and failing a skills test later.
  • ✓"In my research on your company, I noticed [specific detail — product, mission, recent news]. That's part of what drew me to this role." Preparation signals. Study competitors if you can — it makes you stand out dramatically.
  • ✓"What does success look like in this role after 90 days?" A powerful question that shows you think about delivery and outcomes — not just getting hired.
  • ✓"How is the team currently using AI tools in its workflow, and are there opportunities to learn more?" Shows a forward-thinking mindset. Companies actively integrating AI want employees who embrace it.

Things to never say in an interview

  • ✕"My previous boss/company was terrible."Even if true, it makes you look bitter and raises conflict-management red flags. Say instead: "I'm looking for an environment with more [X]."
  • ✕"I'll do anything!"Sounds desperate and unfocused. Employers want specific, passionate people who know what they want.
  • ✕"I don't have any questions."Signals disinterest. Always have 3–5 thoughtful questions ready. Candidates who ask no questions are routinely passed over.
  • ✕"I'm just looking for any job right now."Frame your answer around why this specific role and company aligns with your goals — even if it's not 100% true.
  • ✕Rambling answers with no structure.Use STAR. Aim for 90–120 seconds per answer. Interviewers mentally disengage after 2 minutes of unstructured speaking.
  • ✕"I didn't have time to prepare."Rule one of interviewing: always prepare. Know the company, the role, and have questions ready. No excuses.

Video interview tips (remote-specific)

Remote seekers
  • iTest your tech 30 minutes before.Camera, mic, internet, lighting. A technical failure is avoidable and makes a poor impression before you say a word.
  • iSolid, neutral background.A bookshelf, plain wall, or subtle virtual background. Your environment signals professionalism before you speak.
  • ✓Look at the camera, not the screen, when speaking. It simulates eye contact. Looking at their face on screen makes you appear to be looking down.
  • ✓Have your notes off-screen, not on it.Glancing sideways to read is obvious. Print your key talking points and put them just beside the screen instead.

Strong questions to ask the interviewer

  • i"What does the biggest challenge look like for someone in this role in the first 6 months?"
  • i"How does the team handle disagreements or give feedback to each other?"
  • i"What's the biggest difference between the people who succeed here and those who struggle?"
  • i"What does career progression typically look like from this role?"

Salary Negotiation

Most people who negotiate get more. The offer is the start of a conversation, not a final answer. The employer already chose you — which means you have leverage. Always get the offer in writing before negotiating, and take 24–48 hours to respond.

The golden rule: Never give your number first. When asked for expected salary, say: "I'd love to understand more about the full scope of the role before discussing compensation — could you share the budgeted range?"

Word-for-word negotiation scripts

Receiving a verbal offer — buy time first
"This is great news — thank you! I'm really excited about this opportunity. Could you send the official offer in writing? I'll review it and get back to you within 48 hours."
Countering the offer with market data
"Thank you for the offer — I'm genuinely excited about joining [Company]. Based on my research on market rates for this role and my [X years of specific experience], I was expecting something closer to [10–20% above their number]. Is there flexibility to get there?"
When base salary is fixed — pivot to other benefits
"I understand there may be constraints on the base, and I appreciate your transparency. I'd love to explore other elements — a signing bonus, an extra week of PTO, a professional development budget, or an earlier performance review at 90 days. What's possible?"
Negotiating home office / remote stipend
"Since this is a remote role, I wanted to ask about support for home office setup — equipment, internet allowance, or a monthly workspace stipend. What does the company typically offer remote employees?"

What's negotiable beyond base salary

  • Sign-on / joining bonus
  • Performance review timing (90 days instead of 12 months)
  • Extra paid leave or annual leave days
  • Remote/hybrid work flexibility
  • Home office equipment or monthly stipend
  • Professional development / training budget
  • Start date
  • Equity / stock options (at startups)
  • Health benefits or insurance upgrade
  • Flexible hours or compressed work week

What to never say when negotiating

  • ✕"I need X because of my bills / expenses."Your personal costs are irrelevant to an employer. Anchor on market rate and your value delivered — never personal need.
  • ✕Accepting verbally before reading the full written offer.Every negotiated term must be in writing before you say yes. Verbal agreements are hard to enforce.
  • ✕Apologising for negotiating."Sorry to ask, but..." undermines your position immediately. Negotiating is professional and expected.
  • ✕Giving an ultimatum immediately.Frame everything as collaborative: "Is there flexibility?" not "I won't accept anything below X."

Networking Scripts

85% of jobs are filled through networking — many are never posted publicly. Personalised LinkedIn messages get 35%+ response rates. The rule: be short (under 100 words), reference something specific to them, and end with one low-commitment ask.

The anti-desperation rule: Never ask for a job in a cold message. Frame every first contact as a request for advice, a conversation, or perspective. That gets responses. "Can you help me get a job?" does not.

Message templates that get replies

Cold outreach to someone at a target company
"Hi [Name], I've been following [Company]'s work in [area] — especially [specific project or post]. I'm exploring roles in [field] and would love to hear your perspective on the team. Would you have 15 minutes sometime this month?"
Reaching out to a recruiter directly
"Hi [Name], I noticed you recruit for [Company / industry]. I'm a [role] with [X years] in [specific skill], currently exploring remote roles. I'd love to connect and be on your radar for relevant openings — happy to share my portfolio if useful."
Reconnecting with a dormant contact
"Hi [Name], I hope you're well! I saw your recent post about [topic] and it really resonated. I'm currently exploring [role type] and would love to catch up. Would you be open to a quick call?"
Following up after applying (reaching an insider)
"Hi [Name], I recently applied for the [Role] at [Company] and saw that you work there. I'd love any insight into the team culture or what you'd recommend I highlight. No pressure at all — just thought it was worth reaching out!"
Informational interview (for career changers)
"Hi [Name], I'm transitioning from [current field] into [target field] and your career path caught my attention. I'd really value 20 minutes to hear how you made the shift. No ask beyond that — just looking to learn from someone who's done it."

Networking rules

  • ✓Keep messages under 100 words.InMails under 400 characters have 22% higher response rates. Brevity is respect.
  • ✓Reference something specific to them — a post, a project, a mutual connection. Generic messages get ignored without guilt.
  • ✓Follow up once, about a week later, with a small value-add (a relevant article, an update) — not a copy of the original message.
  • ✕Never send the same message to dozens of people.Copy-paste is immediately detectable. Mass messaging is the fastest way to permanently damage your reputation with a recruiter.
  • ✕Don't connect without a note when cold-reaching.Blank connection requests from strangers have low acceptance rates. Add context — even in the connection note field.
  • iEngage with their content first (leave a thoughtful comment on a post) before sending a connection request. It warms the approach significantly.

Remote Job Strategy

Remote companies hire differently — they can't assess you in person, so your online presence, written communication, and ability to work independently carry disproportionate weight. You need to prove you can operate without hand-holding before you even get an interview.

Remote employers hire for: written communication clarity, self-management, async discipline, tool fluency, and time zone awareness. Signal all five — don't wait for them to ask.

Where to find remote jobs (best platforms 2025–2026)

Remote-first job boards
  • We Work Remotely (weworkremotely.com)
  • Remote.co
  • FlexJobs (paid — filters out scams)
  • Remotive.com
  • Remote OK (remoteok.com)
  • Himalayas.app
General boards with remote filters
  • LinkedIn (filter: "Remote")
  • Indeed (filter: "Remote")
  • Wellfound / AngelList (startups)
  • Glassdoor (filter: "Remote")
  • Working Nomads
  • Jobspresso

Resume — remote-specific additions

  • ✓Explicitly list remote collaboration tools: Slack, Notion, Jira, Asana, Zoom, Loom, Linear, Figma, Confluence. These are the keywords remote ATS systems look for.
  • ✓Mention any prior remote experience — even partial. "Managed project delivery across 3 time zones for a distributed team of 8" is powerful proof.
  • ✓Add your time zone or location to your summary/header.Remote employers plan meetings and workflows around time zones. Make it easy for them: "Based in Lagos, WAT (UTC+1), available for CET and EST overlap."
  • ✕Don't just say "remote work experience."Describe how: "Coordinated daily standups via Slack with a fully distributed team across 4 countries" is specific proof. The label alone is not.

LinkedIn — remote-specific signals

  • ✓Add "Remote" or your time zone availability to your headline. Example: "UX Designer | Product & B2B | Remote-ready · UTC+1"
  • ✓Turn on Open to Work with "Remote" selected as your location preference — it surfaces you in recruiter searches specifically filtering for remote candidates.
  • ✕Don't write "Remote | WFH | Work From Home | Flexible" as your headline.This signals desperation and tells recruiters nothing about your capabilities. Lead with what you do, follow with availability.

Cover letter — remote proof paragraph

Add this as your 3rd paragraph for remote applications
"I've worked remotely [for X years] and am fluent in [specific tools]. I'm deliberate about over-communicating progress, documenting decisions asynchronously, and being reliable across time zones. My work doesn't depend on being in the same room."

Interview — video-specific preparation

  • ✓Test your full setup 30 minutes before: camera angle, mic quality, internet speed, lighting (face lit, not backlit). A technical failure in a remote interview is an avoidable first impression.
  • ✓Look at the camera lens when speaking, not at their face on screen. Camera = eye contact. Screen = looking down.
  • ✓Mention remote-specific strengths proactively: "I'm comfortable working asynchronously and keeping stakeholders aligned via Notion and Slack — I don't need real-time communication to stay productive."
  • ✓Ask remote culture questions: "How does the team handle async communication?" / "What does a typical day look like for someone in this role?" / "How does onboarding work for remote hires?"

Salary — remote-specific negotiation

When location-based pay is brought up
"Based on my research, I'd like to discuss a salary that reflects the value I'll bring to the team rather than being strictly location-adjusted. My output and availability are the same regardless of geography."
Negotiating home office / equipment stipend
"Since this is fully remote, I'd like to discuss support for home office setup — equipment, internet, or a monthly workspace stipend. What does the company typically offer remote team members?"

Local & Onsite Job Strategy

For onsite roles, in-person impressions and local relationships matter enormously — especially in smaller businesses where walk-in applications and direct referrals still move faster than any online application.

Onsite employers care about: culture fit, commute reliability, presence and energy in person, long-term stability, and local ties. Lead with these — they don't need to know about your remote tools.

Where to find local / onsite jobs

Online — local filters
  • Indeed (search by city or ZIP/postcode)
  • LinkedIn (filter: "On-site" or specific city)
  • Glassdoor (filter by location)
  • Google Jobs ("jobs near me")
  • Local newspaper classifieds (still active in many markets)
  • Facebook Jobs / local community groups
Offline & direct
  • Walk-in applications at SMEs (small businesses) — still highly effective
  • Local business associations & chambers of commerce
  • Industry meetups, trade fairs, career fairs
  • Staffing / recruitment agencies (especially for admin, trades, retail)
  • Word-of-mouth via existing personal network
  • Church, community, and professional groups

Walk-in applications — when and how

  • iBest for: retail, hospitality, trades, local SMEs, restaurants, healthcare support roles, admin positions at small companies.
  • ✓Dress as if it's an interview when walking in. You may meet a hiring manager on the spot. First impressions are formed before you hand over a resume.
  • ✓Bring a printed resume — even in 2025. Handing over a physical document leaves a tangible impression that an email cannot.
  • ✓Go at quiet times — mid-morning (10–11am) or mid-afternoon (2–3pm) on weekdays. Avoid lunchtimes, Mondays, and Fridays when managers are busiest or away.
  • ✓Ask for the hiring manager by name if you've researched ahead. "I was hoping to speak briefly with [name] about the [role]" sounds far more purposeful than "I'm here about a job."
  • ✕Don't walk in without researching the company first.Knowing one specific thing about them ("I noticed you recently expanded to a second location") immediately sets you apart from every other walk-in.

Resume — onsite-specific tips

  • ✓Include your city / general location clearly in your header. Hiring managers for onsite roles immediately check whether you're local or will need to relocate.
  • ✓Mention commute capacity or local ties if relevant: "Based in [City], 10 minutes from [area]" removes a common concern for onsite roles.
  • ✓Highlight any local work history — recognisable local companies, well-known organisations, or long tenures signal stability and community roots.
  • ✕Don't list remote collaboration tools as key skills for purely onsite roles. It signals your mind is elsewhere. Focus on in-person teamwork, physical skills, local knowledge, and face-to-face communication.

Cover letter — onsite cultural fit signals

Add this to your fit paragraph for onsite roles
"I'm based in [City] and genuinely excited about joining a team I can work alongside in person every day. I thrive in collaborative, face-to-face environments and am available to start [timeframe]."
Signalling local knowledge (for community-facing roles)
"As someone who's lived and worked in [City/Area] for [X years], I understand the community this business serves and am personally invested in contributing to it."

Local networking that works

  • ✓Attend local industry events, business mixers, and career fairs.In smaller cities and markets, the same people appear repeatedly. One strong conversation can lead to three referrals.
  • ✓Join local professional associations (chambers of commerce, industry bodies, alumni groups). Many jobs circulate internally before being posted publicly.
  • ✓Tell everyone you trust that you're looking.Referrals from personal connections move faster and convert at higher rates than cold applications in onsite hiring.
  • ✓Partner with local staffing agencies for admin, trades, and support roles. They have exclusive access to roles that are never posted publicly and can get you in front of hiring managers faster.
  • iFacebook Groups and WhatsApp communities for local job seekers are highly active in many African and emerging market cities — don't underestimate them.

Interview — onsite-specific

  • ✓Arrive 10–15 minutes early — not 30, not exactly on time. Use the waiting time to observe the environment and pick up context clues about the culture.
  • ✓Be warm and professional with everyone from the front desk onwards. Hiring managers often ask reception staff how candidates behaved while waiting.
  • ✓Signal long-term interest explicitly.Onsite employers fear turnover more than remote employers do. "I'm looking to build something here" is more powerful than "I'm open to opportunities."
  • ✕Don't ask about working from home in a first interview for a role advertised as fully onsite. It immediately signals you don't want to be there.

Freelance & Gig Economy Strategy

38% of the global workforce now freelances. By 2030, freelancers are projected to outnumber traditional employees in several industries. Success in the gig economy requires a different mindset — you're not a job seeker, you're a business owner selling a service.

Freelance success formula: Clear niche + strong portfolio + consistent visibility + repeat clients + financial discipline. Volume of gigs matters less than quality of positioning.

Best platforms by type of work

General freelance platforms
  • Upwork — best for long-term contracts & agencies
  • Fiverr — best for productised, packaged services
  • Toptal — best for top-tier tech/finance talent
  • PeoplePerHour — strong in UK/EU markets
  • Guru.com — project-based work
Niche platforms
  • 99designs / DesignCrowd — graphic design
  • Codeable — WordPress development
  • Scripted / Contently — writing & content
  • SoundBetter — audio & music production
  • Contra — commission-free freelancing
  • Workana — strong in African & LatAm markets

Your portfolio is your resume

  • ✓3–5 strong portfolio pieces beats 20 mediocre ones.Clients decide in seconds. Quality of proof matters far more than volume. If you have no paid work yet, create speculative samples.
  • ✓Write case studies, not just samples.Show the problem, your process, and the result. "I designed a landing page" is weak. "I redesigned a SaaS landing page that increased trial signups by 42%" is powerful.
  • ✓Collect testimonials aggressively.Even a short sentence from a happy client ("Delivered in 3 days, exactly what I needed") builds credibility no self-description can match.
  • ✓Be consistent across platforms.Same bio, same photo, same positioning on Upwork, LinkedIn, Fiverr, and your personal site. Inconsistency signals amateurism.
  • ✕Don't try to serve everyone.A freelancer who does "anything digital" earns less than one who does "email marketing for DTC fashion brands." Narrow niche = higher rates + better matches.

Pricing and rate negotiation

  • ✓Research market rates before quoting.Use Glassdoor, Upwork's rate explorer, LinkedIn Salary, and industry Discord/Slack communities to benchmark your field and experience level.
  • ✓Quote project-based when possible, not hourly.Hourly pricing punishes you for being faster. A project rate rewards your efficiency and expertise.
  • ✕Don't race to the bottom on price to win clients.Cheap prices attract difficult clients with low budgets and high demands. Better positioning earns better clients at higher rates.
  • !Set aside 25–30% of every invoice for taxes.Freelancers pay self-employment taxes that traditional employees don't see. Not planning for this is the #1 financial mistake new freelancers make.

Freelance profile — what wins clients

❌ Generic Upwork/Fiverr title
"Freelance graphic designer available for any design work"
✓ Niche + outcome-focused
"Brand identity designer for wellness & lifestyle startups — logos, systems, and packaging that convert"
❌ Features, not benefits
"I write SEO articles with proper keyword density and on-page optimisation."
✓ Client outcome first
"I write SEO content that ranks — my clients average page-1 rankings within 90 days for 3+ target keywords."

Financial discipline (the freelance survival skill)

  • !Build a 3–6 month emergency fund before going full-time freelance. Dry months happen to everyone — having a buffer prevents panic-pricing decisions.
